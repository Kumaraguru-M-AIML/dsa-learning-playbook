What is Testosterone

🧬Testosterone is an anabolic (growth-inducing) steroid
hormone naturally produced by your body.

📧A hormone is simply a “messenger” molecule, that is
produced in one region of your body, but then travels to another
region, and then “tells” that region to do something. That is why
hormones are often called “messenger molecules”.

📱Hormones are the messengers that your body uses to
communicate with itself, like sending an email or text message
to your friends. For example, when you’re hungry, your gut
releases the hormone “ghrelin”, which travels to your brain and
“tells” your brain to make you feel hungry.

📝So in short, Testosterone is simply a messenger molecule
that is produced in one region of your body (mainly the testes,
ovaries, and adrenals), travels to other regions (such as your
brain or muscle etc.), and then “tells” those regions to do
specific things.

How is Testosterone made

📈Testosterone (T) production happens in 2 main stages,
primarily involving the brain and the testes (or ovaries).

🧠In stage 1, the brain sends a signal to your testes, “asking”
them to produce testosterone. The brain does this by first
releasing a hormone called Gonadotropin-Releasing-Hormone
(GNRH for short) from the hypothalamus (a region of your
brain), to your pituitary gland (another region of your brain). The
pituitary gland then releases a hormone called Luteinizing
Hormone (LH for short), which then travels to your testes.

🍒In stage 2, LH binds to “parking spots” (receptors) on
specialized cells in your testes called Leydig Cells. This then
kickstarts a series of chemical reactions which absorb
cholesterol from the bloodstream, shuttle it into the mitochondria
of the cell, and ultimately convert it to several hormones, ending
with testosterone.

󰞹The process is obviously more complex than this and can
involve multiple alternative pathways. T can also be made in
small amounts in the adrenals (and even in muscle tissue). But
for now, focus on the classical pathway described above.

How does Testosterone
work in the body?

󰞹Testosterone (T)’s actions in the body can be grouped into 5
main stages: production, transportation, entry, conversion, and
action. Keep in mind that this is once again a simplified version.

🏭Production: T is synthesized from cholesterol primarily in
the testes (or ovaries) and adrenal glands.

🚌Transportation: T is carried across the bloodstream by 2
water-soluble proteins: Sex-Hormone-Binding-Globulin
(SHBG) strongly, and Albumin loosely.

🚪Entry: T detaches from its carrier proteins (of if already in
its free form) and enters the target cell or binds to receptors
on its surface.

🔁Conversion: Once inside, T either remains the same,
converts its more potent form called Dihydrotestosterone
(DHT), to Estradiol (the most potent Estrogen), or gets
broken down to inactive metabolites for excretion.

🧬Action: Testosterone (and DHT) finally exerts its effects
on the cell by binding to the Androgen Receptor (AR) or
other receptors inside the cell.

What Should Your
Testosterone Levels be?

🩸Testosterone (T) levels in a man’s body, usually measured in
nanograms per deciliter (ng/dL) or nanomoles per liter (nmol/L),
are constantly fluctuating within a range throughout the day and
week, usually between 300 and 1200 ng/dL (or 10-41 nmol/L).
Free T is usually between 6 to 36 ng/dL (or 60 to 360 pg/mL).

📈This is because your body releases T based on demand,
nutritional and environmental factors. The body also gauges
how much T you need depending on how sensitive you are to it.

🧬For example, due to genetic factors and especially androgen
receptor sensitivity and density, some men may require more or
less T in order to experience same outcome.

🧔So some men may perform optimally at let’s say 500 ng/dL,
others at 700 ng/dL, and others may need over 1000 ng/dL.

🦍But all else equal, a healthy male’s T levels should be no less
than 500 ng/dL and ideally over 700 ng/dL (Free T at least 150
pg/mL) regardless of age. Today, this range (for total T) has
been heavily reduced to as low as 150-750 ng/dL in some
places, in what I suspect to be a malicious attempt to normalize
low testosterone levels in men.

How To Measure Your
Testosterone Levels

🔬There are several ways to measure your Testosterone (T)
levels, using either invasive or non-invasive methods.

🩸Invasive methods involve measuring T levels in your blood,
saliva, or even urine. The most popular of the three methods is
measuring T levels in the blood, often called serum
Testosterone. The issue with this method though, is that it only
provides a snapshot in time, and is costly.

💪Non-invasive methods involve estimating what your T levels
are based on symptoms and signs associated with having low
or high T levels.

🤰Example, it is very easy to estimate what a person’s T levels
are if you know his body fat percentage, waist to hip ratio, waist
to height ratio, BMI (assuming he’s not overly muscular), low or
high T symptoms, or his hand grip strength.

✋Out of all of these methods, the best is by far measuring
hand grip strength. It is cheap, non-invasive, accurate, and
accounts for not just T levels in the blood but also how sensitive
one is to the effects of T, as well as other hormones and
anti-androgens that might counteract T’s actions.

primarily involving changes in men’s nutrition, lifestyle, exposure

and blatant misinformation from medical experts.

male health, reproduction, mood, quality of life, and body
composition.

Protein and Testosterone

🛑Protein (amino acids) is at the very foundation of male health,
performance, and Testosterone production.

🧪Studies also show that low protein diets can reduce testosterone
bioavailability in humans, and its production in animals.

🔬This is because amino acids are needed for virtually every major
biological process in the human body, as well as the synthesis of all
enzymes involved in T production. It is also mandatory for dopamine
synthesis, and for regulating appetite (for estrogen-reducing fat
loss).

📝Eat close to 1 gram of protein per pound of healthy body weight
(or 15-25% of your calories) from nutrient-dense animal sources
such as red meat (beef), eggs, fatty fish (salmon), dairy (milk),
organ meats (liver), and shellfish (oysters).

🚫But, except for rare cases, avoid eating excessive amounts of
protein (more than 35-40% of your calories), as that can actually
decrease T levels.

Dietary Fats and
Testosterone

🛑Dietary fats (combined with adequate protein), are the single
most important macronutrients for testosterone (T) production.

🧪Countless studies show that men who eat low-fat diets have
significantly less T than those on high-fat diets.

🔬This is because healthy fats play an integral role in vitamin

avocado, olive oil), and omega-3 fats (salmon).

as those tend to increase inflammation and offset your omega-6
to omega-3 balance.

Calories, Energy Balance,

🛑Energy balance (caloric surplus or deficit) is one of the
biggest modulators of Testosterone (T) levels.

🧪Studies consistently show that both under-eating and excess
overeating significantly reduce T levels in all organisms.

🔬Under-eating (relative to your total daily energy expenditure)
lowers T by putting your body into catabolic “survival” mode, and
overeating does so by producing inflammation, insulin
resistance, and excess body fat.

📝If underweight or “clean bulking”, eat enough calories to gain
lean mass at a healthy rate (usually a 250-500 calorie surplus
per day, added to your TDEE). If overweight, eat enough
calories to lose weight at a steady pace (a 250-500 calorie
deficit per day). To figure out your total daily energy expenditure
(TDEE), start off by multiplying your healthy goal body weight by
15, and make adjustments each week as you progress.

🚫Avoid crash dieting (losing weight way too fast) or going
overboard on dirty bulks (gaining weight too fast).
