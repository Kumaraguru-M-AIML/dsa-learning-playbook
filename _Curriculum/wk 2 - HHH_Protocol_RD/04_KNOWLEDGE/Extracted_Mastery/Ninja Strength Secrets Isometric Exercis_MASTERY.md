## **CHAPTER ONE**

### ***Kung Fu Fighting***

###

Back in the decade that gave us bellbottoms, platform shoes and white disco suits, something very cool was causing a stir. Its name? Kung Fu. Busy taking the world by storm, there was simply no hiding from this martial arts maelstrom.

To better understand what this Kung Fu universe looked like, fasten your seat belt as we hitch a ride to the 1970s:

* On TV there was *Kung Fu*. This action/adventure series starred David Carradine as Kwai Chang Caine — a Shaolin monk who travels through the American Wild West, armed only with his spiritual training and kick-ass martial arts skills. If you watched the show as a kid, I bet you and your ‘grasshopper’ friends still carry the playtime scars from your schoolyard skirmishes.

* Flying high in the music charts was the disco ditty *Kung Fu Fighting*. Riding the martial arts craze like a surfboarding Ninja, the song would swiftly chop its way to the top of the UK and US hit parade. Anyone who heard the Carl Douglas song would find the catchy lyrics indelibly tattooed on their brain: “Everybody was kung fu fighting. Those cats were fast as lightning.” Winning a Grammy, the song would go on to sell over 11 million records worldwide.

* In comic-books, the Kung Fu influence was everywhere. Magazine giants Marvel had *Shang Chi — The Master of Kung Fu*, and son of infamous Fu Manchu. Then there was my personal favourite, *Iron Fist*. Created by Roy Thomas and Gil Kane, Thomas wrote that he had... “…started ‘Iron Fist’ because I’d seen my first kung fu movie, even before a Bruce Lee one came out, and it had a thing called ‘the ceremony of the Iron Fist’ in it. I thought that was a good name, and we already had *Master of Kung Fu* going, but I thought, ‘Maybe a super-hero called Iron Fist would be a good idea.’” In light of the feverish excitement that gripped teenagers up and down the land, it proved a very good idea indeed.

* And then there were the cartoons, which included Hanna-Barbera’s classic *Hong Kong Phooey*. No one growing up in the 1970s would forget Henry, the mild-mannered police station janitor and “number one super guy!”

But what caused this tidal wave of interest?

Performing a hang-five from its frothy crest was the first international martial arts superstar. His name? Bruce Lee.

## **CHAPTER TWO**

### ***Enter the Dragon***

###

“I will never forget the first two weeks I spent trudging the streets of the Big Apple looking for a partner to publish *Kung-Fu Monthly* in the States. My hair was still halfway down my back. My high-heeled snakeskin boots, Mr Fish tie and Tommy Nutter knock-off suit were not exactly what American businessmen were used to.”

This is the story of how a struggling UK publisher put Bruce Lee’s picture onto millions of bedroom walls. The year was 1974, and journalist Felix Dennis was searching for his first real financial success. Little did he realise this success would arrive with the publication of *Kung-Fu Monthly*, a poster-magazine about martial artist Bruce Lee.

When Bruce Lee died, Dennis sent his friend, Don Atyeo, to Hong Kong to interview anyone who knew the star. These interviews would serve as the basis for the monthly magazine — along with the piles of Bruce Lee photographs that were obtained. Unaware of it at the time, these priceless photos would see Dennis strike gold.

***Empire of the Dragon***

Initially, Dennis’s distributors only gave the monthly magazine a year before it folded. After all, how much could hippy journalists who knew nothing about kung fu publish about one man a dozen times per year? Yet to everyone’s astonishment *Kung-Fu Monthly* didn’t fold after twelve months as predicted. Instead, it continued to be published for ten years.

How did this happen? Well, Dennis had a couple of aces stashed up his flower power sleeve:

* Firstly, Bruce Lee lived fast and died young, thereby cementing his reputation among Hollywood greats like Marilyn Monroe and James Dean,
* Secondly, the martial artist was popular in virtually every country in the world, including the emerging Third World,
* Thirdly, Dennis was passionate about this new magazine format and bravely hurdled all obstacles set before him.

Here is what followed next…

As soon as the magazine was published — it sold over 100,000 copies — Dennis jumped onto airplanes to visit country after country, as he fought to license *Kung-Fu Monthly* to foreign publishing companies. Very soon the mag would have editions in German, French, Italian, Swedish, Dutch, Spanish and Arabic.

When success came, it did so with a flood. One magazine led to another and another; in just a handful of years, Bruce Lee would help build a publishing empire worth millions.

***Picture Perfect***

Posters on walls. That was the secret. The unusual format of a monthly-magazine meant that when kids had finished reading that month’s issue, they could then hang it on the wall as a cheap poster.

And if you grew up in this era, chances are you owned one or more of these posters. The concept was inspired. It was perfect. It also ensured the iconic Bruce Lee physique adorned bedroom walls all the world over.

In a pre-internet age before Twitter and Facebook, martial artist Bruce Lee went viral.

***Feeding Frenzy***

Professor of Computer Science, Randy Pausch, often said, “Luck is where preparation meets opportunity.” This was true for maverick publisher Dennis; in the right place and at the right time, the wannabe entrepreneur would fulfil the kung fu feeding frenzy for over a decade.

So how influential was *Kung-Fu Monthly*?

For ten years Dennis fanned the kung fu flames until they rivalled the scorching breath of the fiercest dragon. Suddenly, everybody wanted to be kung fu fighting like Bruce Lee and own the ripped and chiseled physique of this martial arts master.

This desire would see fans flock to join self-defence classes countrywide, and seek out Bruce Lee’s training methods and workouts in an attempt to look like their hero.

And in Chapter Three, we’ll reveal why the Bruce Lee physique holds up under scrutiny today and has survived to gain iconic status.

## **CHAPTER THREE**

### ***Bruce Lee Body***

###

If you were to draw up a shortlist of the most muscular men in history, martial artist Bruce Lee would likely feature near its top. Indeed, such was Lee’s muscularity, that his physique was described by bodybuilding magnate Joe Weider as “the most defined body I’ve ever seen!”

And the accolades don’t stop there. The Lee physique would go on to influence other bodybuilders, such as Lee Haney, Lou Ferrigno, and six-times Mr. Olympia, Dorian Yates. But what is most remarkable, is how this “Little Dragon” stood 5 foot 8 inches tall in his stocking feet and tipped the scales at a meagre 140 pounds.

So what was the Bruce Lee bodybuilding secret? The answer is quality.

### ***Bruce Lee Physique***

This quality is a primary reason the Bruce Lee physique holds up under scrutiny today and has survived to gain iconic status. Possessing what many consider to be the perfect blend of muscularity, definition and awesome symmetry, Lee would build a body that rippled and bulged as though he were chiseled from marble.

Such amazing conditioning was further enhanced by a very low body fat percentage. How much body fat did Lee carry? Exercise physiologists put the rock bottom essential fat level in males at 3% fat. Such a number is important, as even in starvation, this figure only drops slightly because the body fights to keep its vital organ systems functioning and protected. In effect, a body fat level of 3% (or lower) equals “zero fat”.

To put this figure into context, when author and bodybuilder Clarence Bass had his body fat measured at Lovelace Medical Centre on August, 1977, it was recorded at 2.4%. From this number, we can reasonably deduce that the extraordinarily lean Lee had a body fat percentage at 3% or lower.

### ***Bruce Lee Body Statistics***

In addition to a very low body fat percentage, Lee also showed you don’t have to be huge to create an impressive physique, as evidenced by the following statistics:

* **Chest: 44**
* **Biceps: 14 1/2**
* **Forearm: 12 1/2**
* **Wrist: 6 1/2**
* **Waist: 29 1/2**
* **Thigh: 22 1/2**
* **Calves: 13**
* **Weight: 140 pounds**
* **Height: 5 foot 8.**

Yet these figures only tell us part of the story. What they *don’t* show, is the strength of the Bruce Lee physique.

Indeed, according to those who worked out with Lee from time to time — pound for pound — Lee might have been one of the most powerful men on the planet.

### ***Bruce Lee Body Strength***

1. In 1964, Lee was invited to the Long Beach Karate Championships in California to perform his famous “One Inch Punch”. His opponent that day was Bob Baker of Stockton, who later said: “I told Bruce not to do this type of demonstration again. When he punched me that last time, I had to stay home from work because the pain in my chest was unbearable.”
2. Consider how Lee’s striking speed from 3 feet away was timed at 5 hundredths of a second, and this explains how Lee would often send opponents twice his size flying!
3. At the same Championships, Lee performed repetitions of his 2-finger press ups.
4. Such was Lee’s strength, that he could support a 125 pound barbell at arm’s length for several seconds.
5. For a display of Bruce Lee fitness, the Hollywood star could execute 50 one-armed chin ups.
6. As a demonstration of power, Lee routinely thrust his fingers through unopened cans of Coca-Cola!

We can see Bruce Lee created a quality physique that was strong, lightning-fast, and with next-to-zero body fat. But how in hellfire did he do it? The next chapter holds the key, as we walk the path to mastery.

## **CHAPTER FOUR**

### ***The Path to Mastery***

###

In his milestone book *Mastery*, George Leonard tells the tale of Jigora Kano, the founder of judo. According to legend, as Kano approached death, he gathered his students around him and issued one final request: he asked to be buried in his white belt.

But why would the highest-ranking martial artist of his discipline embrace the emblem of the lowly beginner? Well, the answer is a revealing one. Because for Kano, the journey of mastery never ends.

### ***Your Path to Mastery***

Your path to mastery means giving the best you have to be the best you can be at your most important work. The last part of that statement deserves special mention. How so? Because when you choose to *master the right thing*, then pursuing mastery makes everything else you do seem easier.

Here is why choosing what you master really matters.

I talk about this concept in the upcoming chapters, explaining how simplicity and the principle of Tao can supercharge your training — by focusing on just the one thing, you can witness some powerful changes.

Martial artist Bruce Lee realised this early in his career. And during his pursuit of mastery, he would apply a laser beam focus to his weight training.

### ***Your Path to Elite Performance***

As the benchmark for understanding mastery, we can turn to psychologist K. Anders Ericsson’s study into elite performance. His findings revealed how deliberate practice over the course of years in elite performers made them what they were — elite. Effectively debunking the prodigy myth, Ericsson would show how talent only gets you so far. To *really* succeed takes practice, and lots of it.

But practice alone isn’t enough.

If you want to excel at anything (and this includes your weight training), you must narrow things down to their core elements. Only then, can you take your first steps on the path to elite performance and mastery.

And the progress you make swiftly begins to grow. As your confidence and competence builds, you will make an astonishing discovery: those first steps to mastery are no different from one pursuit to the next.

Why is that? Well, submitting yourself to mastery serves as a platform for other things. In the same way a tumbling domino will knock over further dominoes, knowledge builds on knowledge and skills build on skills. And for *that* to happen, you must develop a thirst for knowledge and become a student of exercise science like 20th century boy Bruce Lee.

## **CHAPTER FIVE**

### ***20th Century Boy***

###

“Move like a cat, charge like a ram, sting like I feel — babe, I wanna be your man,” sang Marc Bolan in *20**th* *Century Boy*. And the T-Rex chart-stormer could have been specially written for martial artist Bruce Lee. Hip, confident and cocky, the 1973 March release would reach number 3 in the UK, swiftly becoming part of the 70s soundtrack.

In light of this, who could possibly predict Lee’s shocking death that same fateful summer?

No-one, is the answer. Why? Because this hip, confident and cocky 20th century boy stood proudly at the zenith of his powers.

Yet Lee was more than a movie star and martial artist — he was also a student of exercise science. Because of this, his thirst for knowledge would result in a strength training routine where each exercise was tirelessly tested for its efficacy.

Until his death in July of 1973, Lee would amass a vast personal library of well over 2,500 books that not only featured the current writings of the day, but included the strongmen of yesteryear, such as godfather of bodybuilding, Eugene Sandow. Such attention to detail saw Lee train all the elements of total fitness, including muscular strength, muscular endurance, cardiovascular endurance and flexibility.

### ***How to Train a Dragon***

This passion to maximise his physical potential saw Lee constantly evolve his weight training. Placing special emphasis on his arms, the martial artist would perform single biceps curls with over 70 pounds and execute one-armed chin ups for 50 repetitions.

More arm training included static contractions (isometrics) where Lee held a 125 pound barbell at arm’s length, and yet more arm training would see the “Little Dragon” exercising with all manner of wrist curls and custom-built gym equipment to work his grip.

Soon Lee settled on a workout that fit with his philosophy of achieving the maximum results from the most economical expenditure of energy. Armed with just a handful of exercises, Lee not only trained his muscles, but would train the strength of the connective tissues such as the ligaments and tendons.

Unlike most of his peers, Lee wasn’t driven by vanity or concerned about the size of the muscle — what mattered to Lee most was training for *function*. With this goal burning brightly in his mind, Lee settled on the following workout:

1. **Clean & Press**
2. **Squats**
3. **Pullovers**
4. **Bench Press**
5. **Good Mornings**
6. **Barbell Curls.**

This routine served Lee well from 1965 until 1970. By now, he had ditched the typical marathon routines that were prevalent at the time, and performed the above exercises for just 2 sets and 8-12 repetitions… training for function would lead Lee to a muscle building workout that was both efficient and brief.

Of all the movements, the squat exercise was the cornerstone of Bruce  Lee’s  barbell  training.  He had dozens of articles expounding the merits of this mass builder, and would perform the exercise in strict and standard fashion.

These same articles championed the benefits of squats and pullovers... and while there is no evidence to suggest Lee ever performed superset pullovers with squats, there is every reason to believe this is likely, as pullovers were considered a productive rib box expander and effective accompaniment to deep breathing squats. Just like J. C. Hise and daredevil Roger Eells before him, Lee would take full advantage of this dynamic duo.

And very soon, this student of science would seek out the ultimate strength exercise, and find it in the safe possession of a circus strongman.

## **CHAPTER SIX**

### ***From Russia with Love***

###

*"I commenced now to think a lot about developing great strength in my fingers… I bent thick, green twigs with my hands, these being better than dry wood, which would not bend much before it would snap. I practiced this a good deal until my hands became so strong that I could bend even small boughs of trees till they broke. Also, I tried lifting stones off the ground with my thumb and fingers, carrying them in my grip for some distance."*

 — Alexander Zass

How many boys dream of joining the circus? Yet for one lucky Lithuanian growing up at the turn of the 20th century, this wish would become an exciting reality.

Meet Alexander Zass. Widely heralded as the godfather of isometric exercise, this soon-to-be “Samson” is visiting the circus with his grandfather. And from all the thrilling sights and sounds he is about to witness, one thing shall stand out and impress him above all others. What is it? The strongman. Holding him in mesmeric awe, it won’t take long for the youngster to make a heartfelt pact: one day, he shall join the circus and become a strongman.

You might think it would be easy to dismiss such childish folly. But holding true to his word, Alexander began strength training immediately. His routine was crude and consisted of such activities as tree climbing, carrying heavy stones, and lifting heavy dumbbells and barbells — yet his efforts would pay off handsomely when he realised his boyhood ambition.

When the day came to join the circus, the wannabe strongman adopted the stage name “The Mighty Samson” — and soon the enthusiastic Alexander was touring Russia with his act. As part of his show, he performed such feats as bending bars, breaking chains, carrying small horses, and even lifting 500 pound girders with his teeth! Yet despite the bravura, the boy from the Baltic never lost sight of those vital elements he considered to be key. What were they? The wrists and hands.

### ***Super-Humanly Strong***

According to the Lithuanian strongman, if you want to become super-humanly strong, you must concentrate on the wrists and hands. The circus performer considered these to be the weak links in many people and that their training often made the difference between a normal man and a strongman. This belief encouraged Alexander to rigorously train his grip, thereby developing the muscles of the forearms and the strength of the supporting tendons and ligaments.

What methods did he use? A fierce proponent of isometric exercise, he would train using handles connected to huge, heavy chains. Explaining the benefits of this training system, Alexander had this to say: “The great secret of developing strength is to do so in a way which will store up energy instead of dissipating it, and build up the stamina as well; and the only method I know which really does this is exercise against very strong resistance.”

Yet events were about to take an alarming turn for the worse. While serving in the Russian army during the First World War, Alexander’s training system would be put to the severest tests imaginable.

### ***The Great Escape***

Reading like a Hollywood script, the next part of our story would happily grace the pages of a blockbuster screenplay.

During the Great War, Alexander Zass was seized by the Austrians and thrown into prison. Confined to a cell and shackled by chains, the captured Alexander feared he would lose the great strength and size he had worked so hard to attain. Out of desperation, he began straining against the chains that bound him and the prison bars that held him. And much to his surprise, Alexander made a startling discovery: this type of training not only maintained his strength, but enabled him to become stronger!

What happened next?

Using just his bare hands, the circus strongman broke his chains, then tore out the bars on the prison windows before making his great escape.

Following his wartime heroics, the “Mighty Samson” later published a bestselling book promoting the remarkable bodybuilding benefits of isometric exercise. Did a copy ever find its way onto Bruce Lee’s library bookshelves? I believe so, as both strongmen shared a passion for this ultimate strength exercise.

And in the next chapter, we shall uncover those principles that make this system so uniquely effective.

## **CHAPTER SEVEN**

### ***Tao of Training***

###

*“The practice of Tao builds daily simplification. Less striving occurs, until there is no striving. When effort is uncontrived, nothing is left undone. One who wins the world does so by not meddling with it. One who meddles with the world loses it.”*

                             — Lao-Tzu (604 BC - 531 BC)

Welcome to Tao: a Chinese concept which roughly translates to the ‘way’, ‘method’, or ‘principle’  —  and signifies the fundamental nature of the universe or natural order of things.

Within these contexts, Tao can also be used to convey the sense of simple, powerful and almost mystical effectiveness.

This simple and powerful effectiveness, is clear for all to see in Bruce Lee’s training philosophy. For example, Lee employs the fundamental training principles — or Tao of training — based on the core bodybuilding moves.

What do I mean by this?

Simply that, Lee makes core bodybuilding moves such as squats, deadlifts and presses the spine of his workout — the same simple exercises that helped build strongman Mark H. Berry and *IronMan* magazine founder, Peary Rader.

And in a moment, I will show you how to apply these techniques  and principles to your training. The result? Dragon-taming routines precision-engineered for efficiency.

To help us on our journey, I must introduce you to a very special razor.

### ***Tao meets Occam’s Razor***

Occam’s Razor is a minimalist law that says the simplest answer is often correct. With a blade as sharp as a Ninja Dragon Star, this principle states that things should not be complicated unnecessarily, and that it is pointless to do with more, what can be done with less.

This amazing law is everywhere you look. Commonly used as a way to slice and dice through any problem or situation by eliminating all unnecessary elements — it even influences the way you see the world around you.

So, can this law be used to simplify your training? Yes. It can. But perhaps more importantly, you might want to ask: “Can this law be used to make your training *more effective?*”

Again the answer is a resounding yes. Here is what you do:

In the same way a doctor would use this principle to determine an illness behind a list of symptoms, we can use the same process with your training. And it is THIS process of deductive simplification which lies at the core of Tao training and the dragon heart of exercise selection.

### ***The World’s Most Effective Exercise***

And now you’ve spied a glimpse of what Tao and Occam’s Razor are capable of, let’s spend some time in the company of arguably *The World’s Most Effective Exercise*.

## **CHAPTER EIGHT**

### ***Beauty of the Beast***

###

If you have any weight lifting experience at all, you will likely deduce which beastly exercise I am about to showcase. On a par with its mighty neighbour, the squat, here is what bodybuilding legend Mike Mentzer had to say about this beast of burden:

“The deadlift is the greatest overall growth exercise as it works every muscle on the backside of the body, from the Achilles tendon to the occiput of the head. Deadlifts also work the deltoids, the forearms, and just about every muscle in the body.”

Impressive, huh? Now what about bestselling author, Stuart McRobert:

“Doing high intensity bent-legged or stiff-legged deadlifts no more than once a week is a big step towards making big gains from these movements, and a big step to injury-free training.”

Later on we’ll check out how to perform deadlifts isometrically, but before we do, let me show you 3 key ways you can shackle this bodybuilding sledgehammer:

### ***Number of the Beast***

1. **Deadlift protocol.** An effective deadlift workout for the average or typical trainee, consists of one single set worked progressively. How often should you perform this beast exercise? ### ***1/4 Ton Bench Press***

### ***Breathing***

There are two schools of thought when it comes to the subject of breathing. In the Pete Sisco camp, he suggests that you blow out forcefully through your pursed lips as you push the bar into your hold position. Other practitioners use this approach too, and I can certainly see the merits. However, for now, I recommend you utilise Bill Starr’s method of breathing which is slightly different.

The rule is simple: you must hold your breath for the duration of an isometric contraction. The reason? If you breathe, your ability to apply maximum force is diminished. So before your lift, take a deep breath and fill your lungs.

### ***The Push***

This next bit is the fun part. This is where you get to explore your untapped strength and test your limits.

Following a thorough warm-up, set the pins of a power rack three-to-four inches below the furthest extent of your reach.  Take hold of the bar and inhale deeply. Now push.

If you were careful and didn’t add too much weight, your bar will travel up smoothly. Now hold the bar an inch or so beneath lock-out. Do not extend all the way up until your elbows are straight, as you will transfer the tension from your muscles to your joints. Remember: we want to contract the muscles.

How long do you hold the bar for in this position?

Just 6-12 seconds ensures you get enough out of the exercise. If you can’t manage 6 seconds, reduce the weight on your bar by 20%, and if you go longer than 12 seconds, add 10% or so to your lift. When your strength begins to fail you, slowly lower the bar back to the pins.

### ***Advanced Technique***

You can also use this system to strengthen your weakest areas in a lift. Still using the bench press as our example, the push from your chest is generally considered the most difficult part of the move.

Here is what you do:

Set the pins in the rack just above your chest in the bench press and perform your isotonic contraction for the required count of 6-12 seconds.

This advanced technique can also help you through “sticking points” in certain exercises. Examples would be the middle third of a biceps curl or the bottom section of a squat.

### ***Iso Tips***

* For isometric and isotonic exercises to be effective, a high degree of concentration is required. Focus intently on your form and the muscles being worked.

* This system is a great way to squeeze in a workout when time is short. Add any push, pull and squat movement and you put your body on the fast track to impressive gains.

* Strive to strengthen your weakest exercise areas. When you do, you’ll continue making progress in *all* parts of your training.

### ***Workouts Tips***

How you structure your workouts and routines is up to you. As I mentioned before, you can either practice your isometrics exercises on non-training days or move them to the end of your normal routine and perform your “holds” then.

Your choice of exercises is easy. Don’t waste your time with any isolation exercises like biceps curls, leg extensions and the like. Instead, focus on the big and simple core bodybuilding moves. Why? Because they allow you to handle the heaviest weights and provide the biggest bang for your bodybuilding buck.

For excellent gains, just three moves will supply you with all the artillery firepower required. What are they? A push, pull and a squatting move. You therefore need look no further than the dip or bench press and pulldown or row for your upper body. Some type of squat variation — occasionally substituted with the deadlift — happily takes care of your lower body.

There is no need to complicate things. Simply rotate these moves and you effectively target *all* the major muscle groups. Your reward? Boosted strength and a lean, muscular body.

### ***Stomach Vacuum Exercise***

Chances are you won’t be familiar with the stomach vacuum exercise. Yet this mighty isometrics move was a staple part of Bruce Lee’s stomach training, and will do more to flatten your tummy than a thousand backbreaking crunches. But before I describe how to perform them, let’s take a moment to tell you what they do.

To better understand their function, picture a woman’s narrow-waisted corset. In Victorian times, these figure hugging garments were instruments of torture capable of trimming tummies to pencil proportions. Now imagine the corset laces being drawn tightly together to pull and suck the abdomen in. As you do, the tummy begins to quickly flatten and disappear. Sounds painful? I agree! Yet there is a simple (and pain-free) way you can achieve this same transformation using the stomach vacuum exercise, and here is how this exercise works.

Firstly, let me tell you that crunches will NOT pull your abdomen in. The muscles of the six-pack (rectus abdominis) run vertically. This presents us with a problem, as the muscle you want to target is the transverse abdominis. The deepest of the six main abdominal muscles, they are composed of fibres that run horizontally, like — yes, you guessed it — a corset. (This also explains how they earned their nickname “the corset muscle”!) Here is why you need an exercise that targets these special horizontal fibres when creating a tight and trim midsection.

The stomach vacuum exercise achieves this by a combination of breath control and muscular tension. The breathing part happens when you empty your lungs, and the muscular tension part happens when you suck in your stomach. Sounds simple? Excellent. Now let’s see how this exercise delivers a Ninja boot to your belly.

Here is what you do:

1. Start from a bent-over position with your hands placed on your knees and your feet shoulder width apart.
2. Breathe in and out deeply three or four times, and on the final count, exhale all the air from your lungs. (This is important, as full exhalation is necessary to fully contract the transverse abdominis muscles.)
3. Now suck in your stomach forcefully as you avoid taking in any air. To help you, imagine pulling your belly button backwards as hard as you can.
4. Continue to hold this muscle contraction with your stomach sucked in for a target of 10 seconds.
5. When you reach your count, inhale fully through your nose then repeat a further 10 times.

### ***The Plank Exercise***

Remember that painful lower back we talked about? Well, I want to share a second isometric exercise with you that works the external oblique muscles on both sides of your abdomen and back. By training these core muscles, not only do you keep them healthy and strong, but you stabilise your entire upper body too.

The plank is more than an abdominal exercise or tummy trimmer. Like the stomach vacuum above, this is an isometric movement that targets your important core muscles, in addition to helping you strengthen your shoulders, arms, and your legs. Why do these two stomach exercises work so well together? Because they train your muscles to stay contracted, the plank and stomach vacuum provide you with a sturdy foundation of strongman strength.

Ready to go? Here is what you do:

1. Begin by lying on your side. Make sure that your legs, hips, and shoulders are aligned.
2. Prop yourself up on your elbow which should be directly below your shoulder. Your forearm will be flat on the floor and your free arm should be lying on your side.
3. As soon as you are in position, lift your hips off the ground. Don’t let your bottom sink and be careful not to hold your breath. Now straighten your whole body like a plank of wood and keep your stomach muscles contracted.
4. Continue to breathe in and out, then repeat this exercise on the other side.
5. If you are a beginner, try to aim for 1 minute while holding the plank position. As you become more advanced, you can either add more time or add sets.

### ***An Interview with Pete Sisco***

***Hi, Pete. It is estimated over 200,000 trainees have used your training methods — how did your Static Contraction story start?***

It started in a commercial gym in L.A.’s San Fernando Valley in 1992. My friend and I started training together. He was a lifetime fitness advocate and I was a busy guy who didn’t like being in the gym and wanted every minute I was in there to give me maximum bang for the buck.

I wrote down every exercise, weight, rep and set that I did and I timed all of it on my wristwatch. After every workout I transferred all the information onto my laptop while we ate hamburgers and yakked about life in general at a nearby restaurant.  We both knew Mike Mentzer and respected his emphasis on ‘high intensity training.’ I studied math and physics and when the talk turned to intensity I wanted to know how it was measured and quantified. Nobody knew. There literally was no measurement being used. (Almost 20 years later, except for my training, there still isn’t a measurement. The mind boggles.)

Anyway, I used a simple measurement of the total weight lifted divided by how much time it took to lift it. Long story short, I called that measurement the Power Factor. Having meaningful numbers led to finding ways to increase the intensity of every exercise and that eventually led to Static Contraction. When you lift under ideal circumstances the absolute maximum weight you can hold for five seconds but no longer, it represents the highest intensity any muscle can generate. Do that on a progressive basis and great things happen.

***Who has been your biggest training influence, and how did they help you?***

My biggest training influences have been, as I mentioned, Mike Mentzer for pointing out the role of high intensity and Isaac Newton and James Watt.

Newton created the new world where people look to science and math for answers to mysteries and Watt pioneered the study and measurement of power. I’m serious about those influences. Newton has probably done more to improve my life (and everyone else’s) than any single person I can name.

Once science and experiment established that lifting heavy things builds muscles in humans, the rest is just the laws of mechanics. For me, that cuts right through 99% of the BS heard in gyms. If someone claims A is a better biceps exercise than B, I want to see measurements that prove it.

***If you were to select the number one training mistake, what would it be?***

People train too often. You can’t grow new muscle until you fully recover from your last workout. Then you wait for new muscle to grow while you are asleep one night. When you go back to the gym you are a stronger man (or woman) and you should be able to lift more weight. When you lift more, you stimulate new muscle growth again. Piece of cake.

But people screw that up by going back to the gym too soon. They aren’t any stronger so they do some dumb workout that hasn’t a prayer of building new muscle. It’s busywork.

This is what happens to people who believe the ‘Monday, Wednesday, Friday’ nonsense. Who on earth can get stronger every 48 hours for months at a time? Maybe a young male jacking himself full of two grand a month in pharmaceutical help could do it. Nobody else can. But guess what days are always busiest at the gym? Sad and avoidable.

***In the same way you might choose an ideal dinner guest, who would be your ideal training partner and why?***

My ideal training partner would be any person who has seriously studied engineering. An engineer has the right training and mindset. He knows you have to have a defined goal and you have to have objective and actionable data points on the path to that goal.

Engineers understand objective measurement. They know that when you deviate away from your goal you have to make adjustments in your tactics.

When people train with Static Contraction or Power Factor they are writing down their performance. Power Factor uses spreadsheets to create graphs so you can see success and failure. Engineers get that.

***If you could travel back in time, what training advice would you give your teenage self?***

I heard every dumb thing we all hear in gyms. ‘Heavy weights for mass, light weights for definition’ and ‘today’s my light day, tomorrow’s my heavy day.’ I’m embarrassed to say I used to repeat it to my friends, trying to be helpful.

I didn’t get smart until my early thirties. Like most people, my visits to the gym didn’t do a thing to transform me. It was only after 1992 when I started measuring everything that I got really strong.

I was amazed at what I could lift. I once lifted a total of one million pounds in just over two hours. It worked out to something like three and a half TONS every minute for over two hours. And I was a guy with an office job who visited the gym maybe three times a month.

***And finally, in your book Train Smart, you say the day is coming when people achieve their optimum muscularity by doing 30 seconds of exercise per month. How do you see Static Contraction Training developing in the future?***

That’s already happening for many people who post comments on my blog. They train about once a month by doing five exercises with intensity that is off the charts compared to most people. Guys who are 60 or 70 are benching over 500 lbs and leg pressing close to a ton. They only do it for five seconds. But when they come back next month they are stronger and can do more. (Technically it’s two months later for the same exercises, since they are on a split routine.)

The big future for Static Contraction will come when a proper static contraction home machine is available. Having a simple machine that uses no weight plates or stacks yet offers literally tons of resistance, records digital measurements of 10 or 20 strength parameters will revolutionise strength training. That can't come soon enough for me.

Nearly every gym today uses machines that could have been built with 1850s technology. I’m serious about that. Take a look at the next machine you use for an exercise and ask yourself what component of it was not available at the time of the steam engine. It’s sad how primitive strength training is in this century. And shameful how many people want to keep it that way for their own financial benefit at the expense of other’s health and wealth.

Thanks for interviewing me, Lee.

Train Smart,

Pete Sisco
